Multi-modal Music Emotion Recognition

Introduction

Music emotion recognition (MER) has become a research hotspot in recent years. As emotion provides semantic information of music, it is the essence feature of music. Music emotion recognition has lots of applications, in recent years, personalized music recommendation systems based on machine learning are very popular, and in order to achieve emotion-based music retrieval, it is necessary to label the emotions of music works. Therefore, research on MER technology to analyze the audio signal of music and to realize automatic emotion recognition of music, has become urgent.

Current researches mainly recognize music’s global emotion, that is, the overall emotion of the music, however, emotion can vary a lot in different episodes of a single music. Therefore, it makes more sense to predict the dynamic emotions of music, which is more difficult and requires more refined methods. There are few researches on the dynamic recognition of music emotion, and fewer on recognition of both global and dynamic emotion. Among them, most of the researchers build two separate models for global and dynamic recognition tasks, which lack flexibility.

Proposed Model

In this work, we focus on predicting the global emotion and dynamic emotion of music as measured by valence and arousal using features extracted from the audio and lyric of the music. Thus, We propose a single model which can recognize the global emotion as well as the dynamic emotion of each second of the music, by adding an extra learnable token to the original music sequence embedding, and utilize the sequential nature of Transformer to predict the global and dynamic emotion of music clips.

Experiments

Dataset: PMEmo contains a list of songs from 2016-2017 Billboard Hot 100, iTunes Top 100 (USA) and UK Top 40 Singles after deduplication, including 794 popular songs. In order to reduce the heavy cognitive burden and improve the stability of emotion annotation, MER-related data sets including PMEmo use music fragments as emotion-inducing materials. The PMEmo data set includes: song metadata, manually excerpted music and human voice clips, pre-computed acoustic feature set, manual emotion annotations, corresponding EDA physiological signals.

Features: In addition to using the music260 features contained in the dataset, we used Musicnn, a model pre-trained for the music genre task, to extract the embedding of the audio. We use Song Key and BPM as static features. In addition, we use a pre-trained BERT model to extract text features from the lyrics.

Framework of the musicnn model for audio features extraction

Experiments: We use Mean Square Error (MSE) as loss function, at training time, we calculate global loss as MSE of global emotion annotation and the t=0 dimension of the output, and also calculate the MSE of dynamic emotion of valid positions, and sum the global and dynamic MSE up as the training loss.

Performance on global emotion recognition and dynamic emotion recognition

Conclusion: The experiment results show that our model is the best among all the other models and it is especially good at recognizing the dynamic motion. Besides, we find that audio features are more important for the accuracy of music emotion recognition.
